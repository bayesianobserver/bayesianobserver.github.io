The problem with the software agents of today is that they are unable to reliably execute a long sequence of (software) tasks to a degree of fidelity that can be called production ready. I think this is because executing a long sequence of actions correctly is a sequential decision processs, similar to the planning problem that is also experienced in other fields where a physical autonomous entity needs to take _actions_, for example:

- Autonomous vehicles
- Humanoid robots

These fields are quite familiar with the hardness of the planning problem: namely that their actions can land them into a state that is unfamiliar, or uncerrepresented, or completely absent from their training data. For this reason, they can go 'off the rails'. That is, they can enter a state from which they do not know how to recover (and having another software agent supervise another software doesnt help because its trained on the same training data). Fundamentally this class of problems is different from the prior geenration of machine learning because they involve _autonomous actions_ on the part of the AI system, and when actions are involved, the system can change its own state, and therefore influence the set of options avaiable to it at future time steps. Simply put, this is a Decision Process defined over, (States, Actions, Rewards). 

In a certain sense, all 3 of these problems (autonomous vehicles, robots and software agents) operate in a closed loop and involve a _planner_. The closed loop is: Perceive $$\rightarrow$$ predict $$\rightarrow$$ plan $$\rightarrow$$ execute $$\rightarrow$$ new state $$\rightarrow$$ repeat.

The planner is responsible for deciding what action to take next to achieve the ultimate goal. Often the planner plans not just 1 step ahead, but an entire trajectory (but does not necessarily take all the future actions in a chosen trajectory, because it can switch to another candidate trajectory). Planning is a complex problem because it requires (a) adherence to rules and ettiquete, (b) making progress toward the goal (c) perceiving ones surroundings and predicting what others might do at future time steps. 

Of these 3 areas, the autonomous vehicles community is the most further along, having realized the above problem a long time ago. The AV literature is clear on the fact that for the purpose of planning, simple prediction of the future, as well as even pure behavior cloning are insufficient. Therefore the planner stage typically tries to combine candidate plans generated by a behavior cloning module, with auxilliary losses that are used for scoring candidate plans on the basis of safety, smoothness of trajectory and adherence to laws. Humanoid robots are starting to incorporate behavior cloning too, and no doubt they too will need to supplement it, to avoid the problem of getting stuck. Ultimately, I think each of these fields that involves a sequential decision process, wil benefit from better world models, so as to be able to reason out of a 'bad state' resulting from covariate shift. 

While early software agents largely relied on next-token prediction without explicit planning, more recent systems have begun to incorporate planning loops (e.g., reasoning–acting–observing cycles and search over candidate trajectories), though these approaches are still far from robust. While next-token prediction and behavior cloning over trajectories provide a useful starting point, they do not by themselves guarantee robustness under distribution shift, especially in long-horizon decision-making settings.
