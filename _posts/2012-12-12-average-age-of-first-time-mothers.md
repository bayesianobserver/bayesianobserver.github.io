The average age of first time mothers in the developed countries of the world has been rising for the last ~40 years.

[![](/images/average-age-of-first-time-mothers-img-1.png)](https://thebayesianobserver.wordpress.com/2012/12/12/average-age-of-first-time-mothers/screen-shot-2012-11-28-at-11-38-27-pm-2/)

Here is another plot that shows the rate of occurrence of Down Syndrome, a chromosomal defect, as a function of the age of the mother at the time of child birth.

[![](/images/average-age-of-first-time-mothers-img-2.png)](https://thebayesianobserver.wordpress.com/2012/12/12/average-age-of-first-time-mothers/maternal_age_effect/)

The curve really starts to shoot up at 30. In the UK, the average age of a first time mother [is 30 years](http://www.oecd.org/els/familiesandchildren/41919586.pdf). It is well known that the fertility rate in women decreases after the age of 30 and drops rapidly after 35. Older mothers are likely to find it harder to have a baby and if they do, then they run a higher risk of chromosomal defects. Given the possibilities of all these negative consequences, the increase in the average age is a bit disturbing. It seems like there is a hidden cost to more women working and for longer.

Why is it that women are waiting longer before having their first born despite the risks? Most of my hypotheses (of which more than one, or none, may be true) have to do with women working:

- Having invested significantly into an education, greater number of women are entering the workforce, with the desire to be financially independent.
- There is greater financial pressure in families for women to work.
- The policies of workplaces in these countries are not favourable to childbirth. I can see this is true in the US, but I doubt this holds for Wester European countries, which I know have policies favorable to child birth.

One source of further information is the following map, showing the absolute increase, in years, in the age of a first time mother, over the last 40 years, state by state in the US:

[![](/images/average-age-of-first-time-mothers-img-3.png)](https://thebayesianobserver.wordpress.com/2012/12/12/average-age-of-first-time-mothers/screen-shot-2012-11-28-at-11-52-03-pm-2/)

This number is highest in the North Eastern states of NY, NJ, MA, CT, etc. The intensity of the colors in the map above correlates well with population density, and with economic activity in general (meaning more working women).  Here are two more plots I came across in a US based [study done by P&G](http://www.google.co.in/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=modern%20motherhood%20p%26g&source=web&cd=1&cad=rja&ved=0CDAQFjAA&url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.pg.com%2Fen_ANZ%2Fdownloads%2Fsponser_of_mums.pdf&ei=Z2bIULuvONDjrAfctoCYAQ&usg=AFQjCNG9PDoSdHnTzmzMw8pbz9FQzz9hwg&bvm=bv.1354675689,d.bmk), that suggest that at least in the US, employer policies may be responsible.
