I recently had the opportunity to read machine learning papers on the problem of designing a good motion planner in the robotics world. Most of these papers were from the autonomous vehicle industry, i.e. from companies like Waymo. I think the set of problems faced in the design of a good planner are quite unique, and distinct from other areas of machine learning. I will use this note to summarize what I learnt. 

Core problem: the main problem can be described as deciding at the current time $t = 0$, what spatial trajectory to follow at various nearby time horizons $$t = t_1, t_2, ... , t_H$$, pursuant to an overall goal of reaching a specific final or intermediate spatial destination. The challenge is that robot is not free to move however it likes: it must move in a constrained geometry (the road network), it must obey certain traffic rules (lane change ettiquette, speed limits, red lights), and it must able to predict the trajectories of other entities (other cars, pedestrians and cyclists) and not collide with them. 

Some terminology: 

* Ego or ego-vehicle: refers to the robot, i.e. the self driving car. 
* Agent: any intelligent entity capable fo motion. The ego is also sometimes called the ego-agent.
* Behavior prediction or trajectory prediction: prediction of the trajectory of each agent, along with associated uncertainties. Note that the future trajectory of an agent is likely to be multi-modal (i.e. having probability density in multiple disparate portions of space or state-space). This is because we do not know the intentions of an agent, and also because the future behavior of an agent is influenced by other agents. 
* State: generally refers to a vector that encapsulates position, velocity, orientation and maybe acceleration $$s_{i,t}(x_{i,t}, y_{i,t}, \theta_{i, t}, v_{i,t})$$.

In the following, I will summarize the key ideas and challenges in this space. Some of them connect to planning in general robotics (e.g. humanoids) while some are specific to autonomous vehicles. 

* Imilation learning: How do we teach a robot what to do in a given situation. One idea is to encode all the information about the current state, the road network, and nearby vehicles into a single context tensor and learn to map this context to what an expert human driver actually did in training data. Using such a model to plan the trajectory of a robot at inference time is typically called Behavior Cloning (henceforth, BC). BC falls into a broader category of ML strategies called imitation learning, where expert demonstration data is collected and used to train a model. A different imitation learning strategy that is not BC, is Inverse Reinforecemnt Learning (IRL) wherein we first infer the objective function that the expert is maximizing, and then at inference time, we plan trajectories that maximize this objective. The problem with pure BC is that it does not generalize well: this is because the state of the robot enters region of state space that are rarely if ever encountered in the expert training data. Since the actions taken by the robot determine its future states, errors can accumulate, and the robot can find itself in a situation that has never been encountered in expert demonstration data. For this reason, IRL is generally considered a better approach, but its not clear to me how prevalent it actually is in industry. 

* Behavior cloning + auxilliary losses: In practice, BC is used to suggest a few baseline plans, and then these candidate plans are 'scored' against several metrics of importance to robotic motion: 
	-- The probability of collision with other agents (which obviously depends on the robot's prediction of these other agents' trajectories),
	-- The smoothness of the planned trajectory. 
	-- A cost related to switching from one trajectory to a very different candidate trajectory, to avoid jitter
