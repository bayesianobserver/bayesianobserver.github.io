The problem with agents today is the same as the problem with: 

- Autonomous cars
- humanoid robots

.. namely that their actions can land them into a state that is unfamiliar, or uncerrepresented, or completely absent from their training data. For this reason, they can go 'off the rails'. That is, they can enter a state from which they do not know how to recover (and having another agent supervise doesnt help because its trained on the same training data). Fundamentally this class of problems is different from the prior geenration of machine learning because they involve _autonomous actions_ on the part of the AI system, and when actions are involved, the system can change its own state, and therefore influence the set of options avaiable to it in future time steps. 

In a certain sense, all 3 of these problems (autonomous vehicles, robots and software agents) involve a _planner_. The planner is responsible for deciding what action to take next to achieve the ultimate goal. Often the planner plans not just 1 step ahead, but an entire trajectory (but does not necessarily take all the future actions in a chosen trajectory, because it can switch to another candidate trajectory). Planning is a complex problem because it requires (a) adherence to rules and ettiquete, (b) making progress toward the goal (c) perceiving ones surroundings and predicting what others might do at future time steps. 

Of these 3 areas, the autonomous vehicles is the most mature, having realized the above problem a long time ago. The AV literature is clear on the fact that behavior cloning is an insufficient form of imitation learning precisely because the above reason. Therefore the planner stage typically tries to combine candidate plans generated by a behavior cloning module, with auxilliary losses that are used for scoring candidate plans on the basis of safety, smoothness of trajectory and adherence to laws. Humanoid robots are starting to incorporate behavior cloning too, and no doubt they too will need to supplement it with something else, to avoid the problem of getting stuck. Onthe other hand, I have not yet heard of software agents employing behavior cloning at all. That is, agents do not seem to be trained on data that takes an (a) objective (b) sequence of actions carried out by a human expert, and use this data as demonstration to a agentic learning framework. Instead agentic models seem to currently be relying entirely on pre-trained language models for planning. My hunch is that this is likely to be insufficient, because agents will keep getting stuck.
